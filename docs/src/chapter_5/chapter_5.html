What is a survey?

A survey is a method to gather information from people

It consists of a series of questions to gather information on a particular subject

Once a survey has been conducted and information has been gathered, they are then turned into data which can be analysed

Digital surveys are faster to launch than speaking with people individually

  • Surveys are a reliable, low cost and convenient research method
  • However, drawbacks of surveys include survey fatigue and potential misunderstandings
  • Ideally you want to capture the authentic thoughts, feelings and interests of your respondents.
  • When a gap opens between the data in your surveys and the real feelings of the respondents its called survey bias
  • There are two main types of surveys: interviews and questionnaires
  • In an interview, the surveyor goes to the respondent and sits with them to ask the questions
  • A questionnaire, on the other hand, is just handed to the respondent and they can answer at their own pace

It is easier to make data using information from close-ended questions

open-ended questions allow more flexibility in response and are useful for exploring new ideas

A multiple choice question is an example of a close-ended question

Close ended questions are easier to analyse

Imagine you are asked to collect information about residents in your town

It might take too much time to collect information from everyone in the town

Instead, we use the concept of sampling

Sampling is a technique of representing the whole population looking at just a sample or smaller part

How do you use sampling effectively?
  • using a big enough sample
  • making the sample representatives random
  • choosing multiple sample groups

Tips to make good questions for a survey

  • Clearly identify the topic you want to conduct the survey on
  • Check for questions that may be misunderstood
  • Choose whether each question should be open-ended or close-ended
  • Avoid leading questions

Types of questions

  • Multiple choice questions
  • Open-ended questions
  • Level of agreement questions
  • Ranked response questions
Putting the survey together
  • Include the survey title at the top of the page
  • Include a brief introduction to your research aim
  • Include a paragraph that identifies you or your group, with your contact infomration
  • Include a concluding statment at the end thanking respondents for participation
  • Ensure questions are as brief as possible and use plain language
  • Minimize ambiguity in the questions and response options
